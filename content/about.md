I grew up in Spain and Catalonia and went to the University of Manchester for
a degree in biotechnology and a master's in bioinformatics and systems
biology. The plan was to do science. Somewhere in the middle of that, I
started writing code for the research I was supposed to be doing and
discovered I was much better at the code than at the science.

That accident turned into a decade in software. I led a small ML-product
team at Unique Insights in Manchester, ran development at a privacy-tech
startup called MADANA in Berlin, and then spent three years at Doc Cirrus
in Berlin building inSuite — practice-management software used by medical
institutions across Germany. The work I'm proudest of there was rebuilding
the container and deploy infrastructure so partner companies could ship
their own apps inside the platform.

In parallel I'd been practising Buddhism since my early twenties. By the
time I left Doc Cirrus the question was no longer whether to ordain but
when. In 2022 I moved to the Dzongsar Khyentse Chökyi Lodrö Institute in
Bir, in the Indian Himalayas, to begin formal study of Buddhist philosophy.
I'm now a fully-ordained Bhikshu in the Tibetan tradition, registered
under the name Tenpa.

These days I split my time three ways. I study Madhyamaka philosophy at
DKCLI and as an academic researcher affiliated with Savitribai Phule Pune
University, on a Khyentse Foundation grant. I write software for
[Offering Bowl](https://offeringbowl.org) — a platform I founded that
lets lay sponsors support Buddhist monastics directly, modernising the
ancient practice of dana for the digital age. And I keep up the
non-profit work I've done for years: building websites for monasteries
and Buddhist centres, supporting charities, and occasionally founding new
ones (Lotus Project brought solar power to a village in northern Vietnam;
SPIN ran a homeless shelter in Manchester out of a former Baptist church).

My writing on Buddhist philosophy and monastic life lives at
[middlewaymusings.com](https://middlewaymusings.com). This site is
deliberately quieter — a place to point people to the work, not a
publishing platform.
